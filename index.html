Brownian Motion

An overview

Brownian motion, also known as pedesis, refers to the random, erratic movement of particles suspended in a fluid (gas or liquid) due to collisions with the fast-moving molecules in the surrounding medium.

The phenomenon was first observed by the botanist Robert Brown in 1827 while examining pollen grains suspended in water. The importance of Brownian motion became widely recognized when Albert Einstein in 1905, and later Marian Smoluchowski, developed a theoretical explanation linking it to molecular kinetic theory, helping establish the atomic nature of matter.

In the background you can see an animation illustrating the principle of Brownian motion. The large gray particle moves chaotically, colliding with other small particles.

Brownian motion is an essential concept across many fields, including physics, chemistry, biology, and finance. It provides insight into the random processes in nature and forms the basis of several stochastic models. In a more technical sense, it serves as a model for diffusion, which is the spreading of particles from regions of higher concentration to regions of lower concentration.

    Key Features of Brownian Motion
  1. Randomness: Brownian motion is inherently unpredictable and follows a random path. Each step of the particle is determined by the sum of many independent molecular collisions.
  2. Continuous Trajectories: Although the particle's movement is erratic, it follows a continuous path without sudden jumps.
  3. Markov Property: The future state of the system (particle position) depends only on its present state and not on its past behavior.
  4. Gaussian Probability Distribution: Over time, the position of a Brownian particle follows a normal distribution due to the Central Limit Theorem.
